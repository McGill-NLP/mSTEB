When a small group of organisms (a small population) is separated from the main population from which they originate (for example, if they cross a mountain range or a river, or if they move to a new island and cannot easily return), they often find themselves in an environment different from the previous one.
This new environment has various resources and different competitors, so the new population needs to possess different attributes or adapt in order to be a stronger competitor than it previously needed to be.
The original population hasn't changed; they still require the same adaptations.
Over time, as the new population begins to adapt to the new environment, it will increasingly resemble the other population less and less. (A slightly more natural phrasing would be: Over time, as the new population adapts to the new environment, it will increasingly diverge from the other population.)
Finally, after several thousand or even million years, the two populations will differ so much from each other that they will no longer be able to be called the same species.
We call this process speciation, which means the formation of new species. Speciation is an inevitable consequence and a very important part of evolution.
