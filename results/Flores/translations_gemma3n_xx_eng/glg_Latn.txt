When a small group of living beings (a small population) separates from the main population from which it originates (for example, if they cross a mountain range or a river, or if they migrate to a new island in such a way that they cannot easily return), they often find themselves in an environment different from the one they were previously in.
This new environment has different resources and different competition, so the new population will need different characteristics or adaptations to be a stronger competitor than it has needed to be up to this point.
The original population hasn't changed at all; they still need the same adaptations as before.
As time goes on, as the new population begins to adapt to its new environment, it also starts to increasingly resemble the rest of the population.
Finally, after thousands or even millions of years, the two populations will have such a different appearance that they will not be able to be considered the same species.
This process is called speciation, which simply means the formation of new species. Speciation is an inevitable consequence and a fundamental part of evolution.
