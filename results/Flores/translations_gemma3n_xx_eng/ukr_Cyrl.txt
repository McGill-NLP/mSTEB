When a small group of living organisms (a small population) becomes separated from the main population they originated from (for example, if they cross a mountain range or a river, or if they migrate to another island and thus cannot easily return), they often find themselves in a different environment than the one they were previously in.
In this new environment, there are different resources and different competitors, so the new species will need to develop new traits or become a stronger competitor than it was before.
The indigenous population hasn't changed at all – they still require the same adaptation as before.
Over time, as the new population begins to adapt to its new environment, they start to look less and less like the other population.
Eventually, after thousands or even millions of years, these two populations will look so different that they will no longer be able to be called the same species.
This process is called speciation, meaning the formation of new species. Speciation is an inevitable consequence and an extremely important component of evolution.
